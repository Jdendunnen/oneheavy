Genre Guide

A Complete Guide to
Metal Subgenres

OneHeavy Josef Den Dunnen April 2026 9 min read

Heavy metal is not one thing. Over more than five decades the genre has fragmented, evolved and diversified into dozens of distinct subgenres, each with its own sonic identity, cultural community and landmark recordings. If you are new to heavy music or simply want to understand the landscape better, this guide covers every major metal subgenre — what defines it, where it came from and what to listen to first.

Thrash Metal — The Speed Revolution

Thrash metal emerged from the Bay Area of San Francisco in the early 1980s, combining the aggression of hardcore punk with the technical guitar work of classic heavy metal. Defined by fast tempos, downpicked guitar riffs, complex rhythmic patterns and aggressive vocals, thrash is perhaps the most influential metal subgenre in history. The Big Four — Metallica, Megadeth, Slayer and Anthrax — defined the genre. Start with Metallica's Master of Puppets.

Death Metal — The Extreme Evolution

Death metal pushed thrash metal's extremity to its logical conclusion, adding guttural vocals, blast beat drumming and increasingly complex and technical instrumentation. The Florida scene of the late 1980s — Death, Morbid Angel, Obituary, Deicide — established the genre's template. Death's Symbolic from 1995 is considered one of the finest death metal recordings ever made.

Black Metal — Darkness and Atmosphere

Black metal is defined by its raw, lo-fi production aesthetic, tremolo-picked guitar riffs, blast beat drumming and shrieked vocals. Thematically centred on darkness, nature and anti-establishment philosophy, the genre's second wave emerged from Norway in the early 1990s with bands like Mayhem, Darkthrone and Emperor. The atmospheric black metal movement later expanded the genre into more melodic and post-rock influenced territory.

Doom Metal — The Heavy and the Slow

Doom metal returns to Black Sabbath's roots, emphasising slow tempos, downtuned guitars and a pervasive atmosphere of heaviness and darkness. Candlemass formalised the genre with their 1986 debut. Death-doom, gothic doom and funeral doom are among the subgenres that have developed from this foundation. Electric Wizard and Sleep represent the genre's modern extreme.

Progressive Metal — Complexity and Ambition

Progressive metal combines the heaviness of metal with the compositional complexity and extended song structures of progressive rock. Dream Theater's 1992 album Images and Words brought the genre to mainstream attention. Opeth's Blackwater Park is widely considered the definitive progressive metal recording — a work of extraordinary beauty and heaviness that remains unmatched.

Metalcore — The Modern Fusion

Metalcore combines the aggression and breakdowns of hardcore punk with the technical guitar work and song structures of heavy metal. The genre has been one of the most commercially successful in modern metal, with bands like Killswitch Engage, Converge and Australia's own Parkway Drive and Polaris building enormous global audiences. The combination of harsh and clean vocals — the scream-sing dynamic — is the genre's defining characteristic.

Symphonic Metal — Metal Meets the Orchestra

Symphonic metal incorporates classical orchestration, operatic vocals and cinematic arrangements into a metal framework. Nightwish from Finland brought the genre to global audiences with their combination of soprano vocals and heavy instrumentation. Epica, Within Temptation and Therion have further expanded the genre's ambition and reach.

Folk Metal — Culture and Heaviness Combined

Folk metal incorporates traditional folk instruments and melodies from cultures around the world into a metal framework. Finnish band Ensiferum are among the genre's finest practitioners, combining melodic death metal with Finnish folk traditions. The genre celebrates cultural identity while connecting with the global metal community.

Groove Metal — Rhythm Above All Else

Groove metal trades raw speed for a mid-tempo heaviness built on syncopated rhythms and crushing riffs. Pantera's Vulgar Display of Power essentially invented the genre in 1992. Sepultura's Chaos A.D. brought a global perspective to groove metal's rhythmic foundation. The genre's influence can be heard throughout modern heavy music.

Finding Your Genre

The beauty of heavy metal's diversity is that there is something for every listener. Whether you prefer the melodic sophistication of progressive metal, the raw aggression of black metal, the rhythmic intensity of groove metal or the cultural richness of folk metal — there is a corner of the heavy music world waiting for you.
